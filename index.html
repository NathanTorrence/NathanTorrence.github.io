Demand for Cycling Infrastructure: Building a Spatial Tool to Fill Gaps in Cycling Infrastructure

A Senior Honours Thesis in Partial Fulfillment of the Degree of Bachelor in Environmental Studies

University of Waterloo

Nathan Torrence


This website proves supplementary content to the paper found here. This project was performed in collaboration with CycleWR.


The pages on this website explore the process of mapping the phenomena of demand for cycling infrastructure and bikeability (as outlined by Winters et al.)12, as well as looking at the differences between the two metrics. More in-depth explanations of the methods used can be found in the full thesis.


Benefits of Cycling


Cycling as a recreational and utilitarian mode of transportation is becoming more prevalent in Canada due to positive health, environmental, and economic benefits10. As an active form of transportation, cycling contributes to the prevention of several chronic diseases, and is associated with a reduced risk of pre-mature death11. Modelling of transportation mode-shares in Adelaide, Australia for example, have estimated that roughly 238,636 tons of carbon dioxide emissions could be avoided every year in the city with a 10% mode-share shift to cycling from vehicular travel13. As well, cycling as a form of transportation is more cost-effective than vehicular or public transportation both in costs to the individual user and in the costs of infrastructure9. Due to the combined adverse effects of vehicular transportation on health, safety, the environment, and infrastructure costs, research has found that cycling can produce substantial economic benefits. In the Netherlands for example, the benefits of cycling translate into an estimated return on investment of €19 billion per year, equivalent to approximately $26.4 billion CAD in 20153.



Infrastructure's Role in Cycling Adoption


While cycling has these broad positive effects, special care must be taken at municipal and regional levels to ensure the successful adoption of cycling as an alternative mode of transportation. Numerous studies have concluded that cycling infrastructure, including designated bike paths, parking, and bike lanes, is necessary for the success of cycling initiatives1,2,4. It is important to note that low-traffic and residential streets can be considered highly suitable for cycling without designated cycling facilities; however larger streets which allow for more direct travel are in higher need of infrastructure7.


In the cities of Guelph, Ontario1 and Portland, Oregon2, research into the role of infrastructure in cycling has shown that individuals who cycle as their regular form of transportation tend to divert little from major road-routes with bike lanes, and that a disproportionate share of cycling occurs on streets with cycling infrastructure. It has also been found that when planning the expansion of a cycling network in a region, it is most important to identify and focus on major routes and destinations where cycling is most feasible such as major business and community centres8.



Demand and Bikeability - Mapping Cycling and the Built Environment


Few methods were found in the literature to represent how the built environment and cycling interact across space. Gaps in cycling infrastructure have been mapped5, though using data such as origin-destination matrices and travel behaviour survey data which are not available in many cities and regions. Other methods6,12 describe how to map the bikeability of an area, representing how well existing cycling facilities and other factors contribute to the cyclist experience. Another phenomenon relating cycling to the built environment is the demand for cycling facilities in a region. While research has been made into factors that contribute to this demand, there is a lack of methods in the literature to map this phenomenon across space.


This project set out to develop a novel methodology to map demand for cycling infrastructure, independent of existing facilities, using data that is commonly available. Such a metric can provide usefulness for better informing cycling policy and initiatives in the region. Used alongside Winters et al.'s bikeability metric12, it can provide further utility in identifying gaps in cycling infrastructure: areas where there is a high discrepancy between how bikeable an area is how much demand there is for cycling infrastructure.
